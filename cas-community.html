Community

Welcome to CAS

The CAS-iRacing Community is a welcoming place for sim racers of all skill levels. Whether you're a newcomer just getting started in iRacing or an experienced racer, CAS offers a supportive environment to improve your driving and connect with like-minded enthusiasts.

cas-community.com
Philosophy

Inclusive & Respectful Racing

CAS values an open, non-elitist atmosphere. The community focuses on mutual support, fair racing, and helping weaker drivers improve. Aggressive and disrespectful behavior is not tolerated — clean racing and sportsmanship come first.

Core Values
Founder

Founded by Andreas Wuschnakowski

Active in sim racing since 2021, Andreas founded CAS out of a desire to create a better community experience for newcomers. His own early experiences inspired the community's welcoming and supportive approach.

Setting

Autodromo Internazionale del Mugello, 5.245 km, 15 turns, Tuscany north of Florence. Ferrari-owned and normally the home of MotoGP. Sim-time set to early morning — dark and overcast, gloomy visibility — giving the whole race a very different atmosphere from Mugello in broad daylight.

Qualifying

Split format as usual: 15 minutes AM, then 15 minutes Pro.

In Pro, pole went to Yannick Wonnenberg (Lamborghini) in 1:44.783. P2 Luca-Maximilian Stein (AMG, +0.308), P3 Mike Zocher (Porsche, +0.364), P4 Dennis Ulli Richter (Aston Martin), P5 Mario Severn (Porsche) — the recently-reclassified Pro who came over from AM after Spa. P6 Lukas Zörlaut (AMG).

In AM, pole was Max Coldron (Mustang) in 1:46.176. P2 Fritz Morawetz (Mustang, +0.124), P3 Michael Krieger (Porsche, +0.287), P4 Justin Christiansen (BMW M4), P5 Klaus Oberländer (BMW M4), P6 Christoph Kiesel (Mustang).

Race

Wonnenberg converted pole into a clean lead and settled into his rhythm at the front. Behind him, Mugello's long straights and fast sweepers exposed every small mistake — and there were plenty. This was one of the chaotic races of the season.

Key incidents:

  • Dennis Ulli Richter — self-inflicted crash after qualifying P4; lost the Aston Martin into a barrier, dropped significantly before recovering to P6 in Pro. "Das hat er selbst hingekriegt," commented the broadcaster.
  • Klaus Oberländer — spun into the sand and ended his race early. A rare DNF for the AM championship regular, costing him significant ground to Coldron in the standings.
  • Mike Zocher made contact with Antonio Cursio's McLaren — Zocher recovered to finish P2; Cursio held on for P6 in AM.
  • Karl-Sören Schober and Mike Girenz tangled in close three-way battles through the middle of the pack; Schober lost time but kept going.
  • Several early DNFs: Marius Becker, Julian Borowski, Thomas Kübler — and Dirk Bolte did not start.

Standout drives:

  • Benjamin Schlosser built genuine pace with the Mustang and ran as high as P3 Pro during the middle phase — the Mustang's Mugello pace surprised a lot of people. Finished P9 Pro after the midfield scrambled.
  • Gregor Micewski (championship leader) had a workmanlike race to P4 Pro from P11 on the grid, preserving his title lead even as Wonnenberg and Zocher scored 40/38 points above him.
  • Benjamin Warnow in the NEON Yellow Audi R8 held off Manfred Baar's BMW to complete the all-NEON AM podium — the team of the day.

🎤 Post-Race Briefing — Mugello R5

The Speed Monkeys’ Wednesday community debrief — drivers review the incidents together, share cockpit perspectives and agree on how to race cleaner next week

Lap 1, Turn 1

The Zocher–Stein contact chain

Mike Zocher drifted left through the opening sequence while Dennis Ulli Richter and another car were already beside him. Two contacts, the Mercedes of Luca-Maximilian Stein spun. Replay review confirmed two clear touches before the Mercedes was turned around — “Glück im Unglück” that the rest of the field threaded through. A red-and-white Lambo further back was shocked, trail-braked, and lost it. Zocher’s likely fault: missed that there were two cars in that gap.

Richter Spin

The follow-up incident after P4 became a spin

Dennis Ulli Richter spun early and, convinced by his cockpit view that he was already mostly onto the grass, let the car roll / touched throttle to clear the track. A second driver, whose crew chief had just cleared the right and called out “slow car ahead”, couldn’t leave enough room in time. Dennis, not on the call, explained the next day that cockpit perspective can be misleading — the rule stands: stay on the brake until the track is clear.

Kerb Save Gone Wrong

A well-meant push that became contact

One driver hit the inside kerb, saw the opponent in front start to rotate, and instinctively gave a small push forward hoping to save the spin. Intent was good; execution was a bit too much. On-stream apology to Christian — cleanly resolved in the room. The takeaway: even in good faith, a nudge on a driver who’s mid-rotation is still contact.

Last Corner

Gear shift too early — Mugello’s classic trap

A solo spin exiting the final corner, same spot as an earlier incident one lap before. Diagnosis after replay: downshift to 3rd far too early, rear stepped out instantly. Andreas pointed out this is typical of motorcycle tracks (Mugello, Jerez, Aragon): low-downforce, high-speed setups are pointy. One brake marker too deep or one gear too early and the car is around. Good one to study on the replay.

📋 Full briefing notes

The bigger debate: how should you react to an accident ahead?

The longest discussion of the evening. One driver pointed out a recurring pattern: in the same danger situation, five cars around them will all back off, and one will just plough through — sometimes gaining places as a result. “Are we being too naive by lifting?”

The consensus after twenty minutes of honest back-and-forth:

  • We’re not robots. Different ages, different reaction times, different experience levels — some drivers race the sim five days a week, some bought the track on Sunday and are seeing it for the first time on Tuesday.
  • The right reaction isn’t “brake panic” and it isn’t “flat through.” The target is lift, look, check — confirm where the incident is before committing.
  • The league can’t legislate a uniform reaction across 33 drivers. What it can do is sanction clear yellow-flag violations when they’re protested.
  • If someone repeatedly uses danger situations to gain position — report it. Even a first offence is worth submitting, just to get it on record.

New: blue-flag enforcement tool (pilot)

iRacing itself shows a blue flag at ~1.5 s — far too late for a race league. The new tool CAS is piloting enforces the league’s 0.5 s threshold automatically:

  • Stage 1 (live in Mugello): a whisper message to the driver being caught, telling them to let the lapper through.
  • Stage 2 (not yet active): automatic penalty after a further ~10 s of non-compliance.
  • Stage 2 will only go live if two-thirds of WCT drivers vote in favour in the WCT Info poll currently open. Please vote.

Light-flashing and lapping etiquette

One driver reported being stupidly rear-ended by someone 1–1.5 s per lap faster — the first time in a long while. Others agreed some drivers are over-aggressive with headlight flashes. Andreas: in non-esports leagues, a single flash to signal an overtake is fine; permanent flashing is intimidating and causes the slower driver to make a mistake — which is exactly what you don’t want.

Practical tip if you’re being lapped: lift before the braking zone, hold your own racing line, let the faster car have the full exit with slipstream. Trying to dive off-line mid-corner costs both of you more time.

Protest workflow reminder

  • Report in the CAS system — even first-offence situations can be submitted.
  • Always include the replay timestamp. Andreas added a diagram in the regulations under “submitting protests” showing exactly where to find it.
  • Protests are judged by context (speed on the prior lap, brake points, all camera angles) — the stewards want to understand what happened, not just punish.

The spirit of the briefing

Andreas closed with the point the whole format exists for: this is not an accusation tribunal. Drivers reviewed their own footage, admitted when they were at fault, apologised where needed, and moved on. Season 12 feels calmer than past seasons because most of the grid is returning — people know how each other races, and the 1–3-race “getting-to-know-you” phase is behind us. The Wednesday briefing is quickly becoming a fixture of the CAS community calendar.

Championship

🏅️ Silvio doubles up — lead grows

Before Monza, Silvio led the standings on 228 points, André Rajkovic on 199. After sweeping both races at Monza, Silvio extends that cushion further with only four rounds to go. Fastest lap of the day: Willi Brandt, 1:47.1 — with Silvio and Rajkovic within a tenth of each other on pure pace.

Next round: Barcelona GP, Wednesday 7 May 2026

📋 Full race report

Setting

Autodromo Nazionale di Monza — the 1922 original, the Temple of Speed. Low-wing setups, huge slipstream on the straights, but three demanding chicanes that punish the smallest mistake. 12 drivers on the grid, clear weather, no rain in the forecast.

Qualifying

10-minute session, 3 timed laps. Early benchmark from Silvio (1:47.7), before Willi Brandt briefly took top spot with 1:47.6 — then ran wide and lost the lap. In the closing minutes André Rajkovic laid down a cleaner second run to take pole. Starting grid: Rajkovic, Silvio, Brandt, Klaus Oberländer, Benjamin Schlosser, Andy Weber, Thomas Felix, Christian Schlosser, Olli, Sean Pfennig, Jan Tobias Schrader, Ricardo Cavoto. Two-second spread from pole to P12 — a tight field.

Race 1 — 25 minutes, standard grid

Clean standing start — all 12 cars through the first two chicanes unscathed, a welcome change after the GT3 WCT chaos 48 hours earlier. The front four (Rajkovic, Silvio, Brandt, Oberländer) ran inside one second of each other for most of the first half. Sean Pfennig lost the tow early and dropped out of the lead group; a solo spin elsewhere in the pack claimed an early retirement.

The race-defining moment came in a three-wide battle into the second chicane. Silvio, Rajkovic and Willi Brandt were side-by-side entering the braking zone — Brandt’s inside dive connected lightly with Rajkovic, who spun. Broadcaster’s read: “unintentional, but a clear racing incident.” Silvio escaped with a clean run out of the chicane and a 3.7 s gap; Rajkovic dropped to P6. Brandt was arguably punished more by the consequences than Rajkovic — he lost time too and fell behind Andy Weber.

From there Silvio managed the race from the front. Benjamin Schlosser capitalised to grab P2, with Rajkovic fighting back to P3 by the end. Brandt recovered to P5 after a late pass on Oberländer into the Parabolica.

Race 1 result: Silvio, Schlosser, Rajkovic, Oberländer, Brandt, Weber, Pfennig, Christian Schlosser, Olli, Felix, Schrader, Cavoto.

Race 2 — 25 minutes, reverse-grid top 8

With Ricardo Cavoto (P12 in R1) lining up on pole and the quick drivers further back, Monza’s long straights teed up exactly the kind of charging race the reverse-grid format is built for. Clean through the first chicane again — another strong start from a very disciplined field.

Jan Tobias Schrader took the lead from Cavoto. Silvio, Brandt and Rajkovic began carving through the midfield on the power sections. Rajkovic worked his way into the lead over the first ten minutes, before the unstoppable Silvio caught up and retook P1 with a slipstream pass on the start-finish straight.

The single big incident of Race 2 was at the Parabolica, where Andy Weber and Olli Utz went side-by-side on entry. Weber moved across without seeing Olli on the inside — contact, both cars compromised. Broadcaster’s read (not a stewards’ call): fault with Weber, most likely a spotter gap rather than intent. Olli still recovered to P7; Weber dropped to P10.

A brief internet dropout on the broadcaster’s end (Thomas streaming from France) caused a short stream outage, during which Schrader passed Brandt for track position. The stream came back in time for the final fight: Silvio clear of Rajkovic by about a second, Brandt reeling Schrader in but running out of laps.

Race 2 result: Silvio, Rajkovic, Brandt, Schrader, Cavoto, Oberländer, Olli, Benjamin Schlosser, Christian Schlosser, Weber, Pfennig, Felix.

Notes from the booth

  • Fastest lap overall: Willi Brandt, 1:47.1, with Silvio (1:47.2) and Rajkovic (1:47.3) a breath behind — the top three were separated by two tenths on pure pace.
  • Monza rewarded clean sectors over risk. Several close overtakes were called off because the front driver held a good line — the speaker’s observation that Monza “looks easy to pass on paper, but if the lead car runs clean you barely get by.”
  • Trophies for the full season have been ordered by organiser Andreas — they’re on their way.
  • Next round: Barcelona GP, Wednesday 7 May 2026. Then Silverstone GP, Hockenheim GP, and Okayama (Full Course) to close the season.

2026 Champion

🏅️ Matthias Bär — new TSS GT4 Champion

Bär seals Season 3 with a controlled P3 finish, dethroning defending champion Pitt Vinanczi. The two had run nose-to-tail at the front for most of the race; in the closing minutes Bär backed out of the fight to bank the title without risk — even letting Eckert through for P2 on track.

Final championship: Bär — Vinanczi — Eckert — Raab

Strategy of the Day

🔥 Eckert’s half-set tyre gamble

With the track at 53°C and rubber dying lap by lap, Philipp Eckert made a last-minute call in the pits to change only the left-side tyres — front-left and rear-left. The longer stop dropped him on track, but in the second half he ran 1:26 lap times nobody else could match, ate up the gap and overtook Raab into the chicane to claim P2.

Vinanczi’s win came on full fuel-saving discipline — he crossed the line with just 0.1 litre left.

📋 Full race report

Setting

Road Atlanta — 4.06 km, fast and undulating. Air temperature 22°C, but a track that climbed past 53°C by lights-out turned the entire race into a tyre-and-fuel management exercise — the cars “driving like on soap,” in the broadcaster’s words. Eight drivers on the grid; many regulars sat the finale out once the championship picture was clear.

Pre-race notes

  • Kevin Hilkenhöfel removed from the league after the unsporting incident with Pitt Vinanczi at the previous round; matter is closed.
  • Andi (cas_sim_tv commentator) announced this would be his last commentary for several weeks — he sets out on 12 May for a 3,500 km bicycle ride to North Cape (estimated 8–10 weeks). Andreas will cover commentary in the meantime.

Qualifying

10-minute Lone Quali. Late drama: Jürgen Raab held provisional pole through most of the session and looked set to take it — until Matthias Bär delivered a final-lap masterclass (mirroring exactly what he did at the previous round) to snatch P1 by 0.066 s. Starting grid: Bär, Raab, Eckert, Vinanczi, Kübler, Olli Utz, Cavoto, Felix.

Olli Utz had a technical issue and started from the pit lane.

Race — first stint

At lights-out, Vinanczi made the move of the day on the run to Turn 1: he passed both Raab and Eckert in one go, jumping from P4 to P2 behind Bär. Light contact between Raab and Eckert in the squeeze, but both kept going.

Olli Utz lost the rear coming out of the pits and crashed heavily into the barrier — race over for him on the spot, although he later returned to record a finishing position.

The lead trio — Bär, Vinanczi, Eckert — settled into a tight group, with Vinanczi sitting deliberately in Bär’s tow, saving fuel and tyres rather than attacking. Raab dropped out of slipstream range to P4 within ten minutes. Ricardo Cavoto kept understeering off-track on dying tyres and lost time with multiple slow-down zones; Thomas Kübler eventually had a 1:10-minute pit stop and dropped to the back.

The pit phase — tactics decide the race

  • Jürgen Raab pitted first, attempting an undercut on Eckert. It worked — he stopped 1.7 s shorter and emerged ahead of Eckert on track for P3.
  • Eckert took the gamble that defined the race: change only the left-side tyres (front and rear). Longer stop, but the new rubber would prove decisive.
  • Bär and Vinanczi pitted together in the same lap. Neither changed tyres. Vinanczi spent slightly less time stationary — thanks to his fuel-saving first stint — and emerged in P1, swapping the lead with Bär.

Race — second stint

The strategy unwound on track. Eckert, on his fresh left-sides, started clocking 1:26 lap times nobody else could match, devouring the gap to Raab. Caught him a couple of laps later, dived inside into the chicane, ran side-by-side through the next right and was through for P3.

Raab’s race then turned bad: a self-inflicted crash into the wall earned him a meatball flag and dropped him out of the leading group. He recovered to finish P4.

Eckert kept charging towards the leaders, turning the front fight into a three-way scrap. With three minutes left, Bär — the new champion-elect — visibly backed out: a fuel-saving lap that may also have been a deliberate decision to avoid risk. He let Eckert through for P2 and cruised home a champion in P3.
